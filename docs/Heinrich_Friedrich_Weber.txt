<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heinrich_Friedrich_Weber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Around 1861 he entered the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Jena" title="University of Jena">University of Jena</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Abbe" title="Ernst Abbe">Ernst Abbe</a> became the first of two <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicists</a> who decisively influenced his career (Weiss 1912, pp.&#160;44&#8211;45). Weber soon discovered, however, that he lacked sufficient mathematical talent, and so he abandoned mathematics entirely (Weiss 1912, p.&#160;44).
</p><p>Returning to physics, Abbe found in Weber a young and dynamic scientist, one who successfully focused much of his research efforts on re-thinking optical theory. Abbe not only instructed Weber in the lecture hall and laboratory, he also served as a role model for him in several other ways: through his emphasis on the importance of laboratory work in general and precision instrumentation in particular; through his view that science should be closely related to practical life; and through his embodiment of the idea that a single individual could accomplish much in life. This last point, according to <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Weiss" title="Pierre Weiss">Pierre Weiss</a>, Weber’s obituarist and successor at the <a href="/wiki/Eidgen%C3%B6ssische_Technische_Hochschule" class="mw-redirect" title="Eidgenössische Technische Hochschule">Eidgenössische Technische Hochschule</a>, as the Zurich Polytechnic came to be called after 1911, was “the mainspring of his [Weber’s] life, the source of his most beautiful successes” (Weiss 1912, p.&#160;44).
</p><p>Weber received his doctorate under Abbe in 1865 with a dissertation on the theory of light <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a>. He spent the second half of the 1860s as a <a href="/wiki/Privatdozent" title="Privatdozent">private tutor</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pforzheim" title="Pforzheim">Pforzheim</a>, publishing only one article during this period. Pforzheim was close to both the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Heidelberg" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Heidelberg">University of Heidelberg</a>, where Weber came into contact with <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Kirchhoff" title="Gustav Kirchhoff">Gustav Kirchhoff</a>, one of the leading theoretical physicists of the day, and to the Polytechnische Schule in <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe" title="Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe</a>, where in 1870 he became <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Heinrich_Wiedemann" title="Gustav Heinrich Wiedemann">Gustav Heinrich Wiedemann</a>’s assistant (Weiss 1912, p. 45). At the same time, Weber also managed to meet the professor of physiology at Heidelberg, <a href="/wiki/Hermann_von_Helmholtz" title="Hermann von Helmholtz">Hermann von Helmholtz</a>, perhaps through Kirchhoff or Wiedemann, since both of them were close friends of Helmholtz’s. When Helmholtz left <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a> in 1871 to accept the call as professor of physics at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Berlin" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Berlin">University of Berlin</a>, he took Weber along as his first assistant. Helmholtz now became the second formative, and decidedly primary, influence on Weber’s career.
</p><p>During his three years as Helmholtz’s assistant in Berlin (1871&#8211;1874), Weber helped Helmholtz set up and equip the Berlin laboratory and also helped him direct the student laboratories. 
</p><p>In 1872 and 1874, Weber published two noteworthy papers in the <i><a href="/wiki/Annalen_der_Physik" title="Annalen der Physik">Annalen der Physik</a></i> on determining the <a href="/wiki/Specific_heat" class="mw-redirect" title="Specific heat">specific heats</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> at various temperatures (Weber 1872, 1874).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Working in Helmholtz’s refurbished Berlin laboratory, Weber measured the specific heats of these three elements and showed them to be noticeably smaller at low temperatures than predicted by the <a href="/wiki/Dulong%E2%80%93Petit_law" title="Dulong–Petit law">Dulong&#8211;Petit law</a>; moreover, he found that, with an increase in temperature, their specific heats increased extraordinarily rapidly. Only when he increased the temperature beyond 1,000 degrees Celsius did the specific heats again follow the predictions of Dulong and Petit. For nearly thirty years Weber’s empirical findings remained an anomaly until one of his students presented a new explanation (Weiss 1912, pp. 49&#8211;50; Pais 1982, pp. 391&#8211;392).
</p><p>Weber married Anna Hochstetter in 1875. The couple had three daughters and five sons, all of which became academics: Oskar: chemist; Friedrich: geologist; Ernst: civil engineer and astronomer; Helmut and Richard: physicians.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Einstein_and_Weber">Einstein and Weber</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heinrich_Friedrich_Weber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Einstein and Weber">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> considered Weber a doctoral advisor. Following a bitter disagreement with Weber, Einstein switched to <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Kleiner" title="Alfred Kleiner">Alfred Kleiner</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  Heinrich Weber was both Einstein's and <a href="/wiki/Mileva_Mari%C4%87" title="Mileva Marić">Mileva Marić</a>'s thesis advisor, and he gave their respective papers the two lowest essay grades in the class, with 4.5 and 4.0, respectively, on a scale of 1 to 6.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Einstein also famously once commented that Weber's lectures were 50 years out of date and did not contain <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations" title="Maxwell&#39;s equations">Maxwell's equations</a>.
</p>
